Abstract

Hierarchical navigation systems typically rely on low-level controllers to compensate for disturbances or modeling uncertainties, while planning decisions are made using simplified nominal models. Under non-negligible Uncertainties, such separation can lead to dynamically infeasible motion and degraded closed-loop performance. This paper presents a disturbance-aware Integrated Trajectory Planner and Controller (ITPC) that explicitly accounts for uncertainties at the dynamics level. Given only waypoints from arbitrary path planners (e.g., A*, Vision-Language-Action methods), the proposed framework directly generates control commands by jointly reasoning over full-order system dynamics with estimated disturbance considering motion progression, without requiring time-parameterized reference trajectories or predefined velocity profiles. To enable real-time operation on resource-constrained platforms, we introduce a variable discretization strategy that extends the effective prediction horizon while maintaining low computational cost. This allows the system to adapt its motion in response to environmental disturbances rather than tracking a fixed nominal trajectory. Extensive simulations and hardware experiments demonstrate that ITPC achieves improved robustness and safety compared to representative hierarchical baselines, while remaining computationally efficient on resource-constrained platforms with limited CPU-only computational resources.

Contributions

  1. Disturbance-aware integrated planning and control. A unified optimization layer directly maps waypoints to control commands under state and disturbance uncertainties.
  2. Computationally efficient long-horizon optimization. A variable discretization strategy enables real-time performance on resource-constrained platforms.
  3. Extensive real-world validation. Simulation and hardware experiments demonstrate improved robustness and safety over representative baselines.

Methods

This section presents the proposed ITPC. Rather than introducing independent algorithmic components, the results are organized to reflect a coherent progression from disturbance-aware state estimation, through computationally efficient horizon discretization, to an integrated planning and control architecture. We first introduce a FR-MHE that enables real-time joint state/disturbance estimation under tight computational constraints. Building upon this estimator, we further tackle the resulting increase in computational complexity induced by integrated design via proposing a VDG strategy, which preserves long-horizon foresight while maintaining tractable online computation. Finally, we integrate FR-MHE and VDG into a unified sampling-based MPC framework, yielding the ITPC that jointly reasons about planning and control at the dynamics level with merely waypoints as its input. Together, these results establish a disturbance-aware, computationally efficient, and fully integrated navigation framework that departs from conventional hierarchical designs.

We consider three configurations. Note that in this comparison, log-MPPI serves as both the local trajectory planner and the cascaded controller.

  1. A*+MPPI: path planner + log-MPPI with fixed velocity.
  2. A*+miniS+MPPI: path planner + global trajectory planner + log-MPPI.
  3. A*+ITPC: path planner + proposed ITPC.

Under nominal conditions, the hierarchical pipeline with minimum-snap planning (case (ii)) achieves the optimal performance due to the time-parameterized global trajectories when disturbances are mild. In contrast, the fixed-velocity baseline (case (i)) exhibits a very low success rate and is therefore omitted from further quantitative discussion. This failure is primarily caused by the aggressive safety radius, which pre- vents the controller from recovering once the system deviates from the nominal path.

When wind disturbances are introduced, the limitations of hierarchical designs become evident. In contrast, the proposed ITPC maintains a 90% success rate under the same conditions, despite only one trial failing to a local minimum stuck case due to the sampling nature of MPPI algorithm, as depicted in Fig. 7.

To further examine the potential of the proposed integrated planning and control, we remove the velocity constraint in ITPC. As shown in Fig. 8, the system achieves smoother and more agile navigation, reaching a maximum speed of 5.26 m/s with a 100% success rate over ten trials. This result demonstrates that eliminating rigid velocity prescriptions and allowing planning decisions to emerge from dynamics- level optimization can unlock additional performance gains, reinforcing the benefits of the proposed integration paradigm.
